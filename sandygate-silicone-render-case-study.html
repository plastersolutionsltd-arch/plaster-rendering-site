The Project

Premium Full Silicone Sandygate.

This Sandygate property — a detached home in one of Sheffield's most desirable S10 neighbourhoods — came to us for our premium full silicone system. The client wanted the best: zero compromise on materials, zero shortcuts on process, and a finish that would last decades without any maintenance.

We specified Ecorend Marble White — our go-to premium silicone product. Self-cleaning, hydrophobic, UV-stable through-colour with a 25-year manufacturer warranty. At 1.5mm scraped texture, Marble White gives a clean, classic finish that stays bright through Sheffield's wet winters without moss, algae, or staining. Exactly the right choice for Sandygate.

At 120m² across a full 3-week programme, this job was documented at every stage — from the first bead going on the wall to the finished coat. Every stage photographed, every decision explained below.

Stage by Stage

Every Stage Documented.

Swipe or use the arrows to move through each stage. More stages will be added as the job progresses.

Stage 01 / 22 Beading
Stage 01 — Render Bead Installation

Setting the Framework

Before any render touches the wall, every bead is fixed. This overview shot shows the render stop beads running along the base of the walls and up the corners — set perfectly level and plumb. These control the render thickness across the full 120m² and ensure the system terminates cleanly at every edge. The scaffold is up, windows and doors protected, and the property is ready for render.

Stage 01 / 22 Window Reveals
Stage 01b — Window Reveal Beading

Window Reveal Detail

Every window reveal is beaded with stop beads fixed to all four sides — top, sill, and both jambs. The window frame is fully masked with plastic sheeting and blue tape so no render contacts the frame. This level of protection means a clean, sharp reveal on the finished coat with zero overspray. A proper bead here is what separates a professional render from an amateur one — the reveals are always where the quality shows.

Stage 01 / 22 Door Reveals
Stage 01c — Door Reveal & Base Bead

Door Reveal & Perimeter Beading

The door reveal is beaded on both jambs with the door frame fully wrapped and protected. Note the base bead running along the bottom of the wall at DPC level — this controls the lower termination of the render system and prevents water tracking back under the edge. The substrate here is exposed brick: sound, dry, and ideal for direct render application. No hacking off, no remedial work needed — a clean slate for the base coat.

Stage 02 / 22 Base Coat
Stage 02 — Base Coat Application

First Coat on the Gable

Ecorend polymer-modified base coat goes on. This is the structural layer of the system — it bonds directly to the render boards and provides the foundation for the mesh reinforcement. You can see the Ecorend board joints through the fresh coat; once the mesh is embedded and the coat cures, the surface will be uniform and solid. The orange stop bead at the base controls the render thickness and creates a clean termination at DPC level.

Stage 02 / 22 Base Coat
Stage 02b — Rear Elevation

Full Wall Coverage

The rear elevation taking its base coat. At this stage the priority is even coverage across the full wall — consistent thickness, no holidays, good adhesion at all edges and corners. The render boards' joints are visible through the coat, which is normal at this stage. Once cured, the mesh layer will fully bridge those joints and the finished coat will show no trace of them. The scaffold gives full access to work top to bottom in one continuous pass.

Stage 02 / 22 Base Coat
Stage 02c — Curing in Good Conditions

Base Coat Setting Up

A rare Sheffield sunny day — ideal curing conditions. The base coat needs to set fully before the mesh is embedded and the next coat applied. Rushing this stage is one of the most common causes of render failure; the base needs to be firm but not bone-dry. Temperature and humidity are monitored throughout — on a product like Ecorend, you follow the data sheet exactly. The scaffold shadow gives a clear sense of the height and scale of work on this elevation.

Stage 02 / 22 Mesh Detail
Stage 02d — Corner Mesh Reinforcement

Mesh Embedded at the Corner

This is the most critical detail in the whole system — the corner. Alkali-resistant fibreglass mesh is pressed into the wet base coat and wrapped fully around the corner, with additional diagonal patches at the top and bottom of each window and door. Corners are where buildings move most; they're where untreated renders crack first. This mesh wrapping eliminates that risk completely. Every corner on this Sandygate job was treated the same way — no shortcuts.

Stage 02 / 22 Base Coat
Stage 02e — Application in Progress

Wet and Setting

Close-up of the base coat mid-application — the right side freshly applied, the left already beginning to set. You can see the difference in colour and sheen as the product cures. The scaffold pole runs through the working area; access management is part of the job on a tight elevation like this. The base coat is worked in sections to maintain a wet edge, ensuring there are no cold joints where sections meet. This is the kind of detail that makes a 25-year system, not a 5-year one.

Stage 02 / 22 Base Coat
Stage 02f — Working Platform

The Setup Behind the Finish

The flat roof working platform on this extension — buckets mixed, tools laid out, the freshly coated gable wall above. This shot shows the scale of the working area and why scaffold access on this property required careful planning. The yellow bucket holds freshly mixed Ecorend base coat; the white bucket is the water supply for consistency. Every batch is mixed to the same ratio. Above, the base coat is curing in good conditions — ready for mesh and the next stage.

Stage 02 / 22 Ruling Up
Stage 02g — Ruling Up

Pulling the Straight Edge

The action shot — a long straight edge (darby rule) being drawn across the base coat to rule it up flat and level. You can clearly see the difference between the rough, freshly applied coat on the left and the flat, ruled surface on the right where the straight edge has passed. This is how the base coat is brought to a consistent thickness across the full elevation — any high spots are knocked back, any low spots filled. This is the skill. The flat surface this creates is what allows the silicone topcoat to go on perfectly uniform.

Stage 02 / 22 Keyed Surface
Stage 02h — Air Bubble Removal

Working Out the Air

This is a critical but rarely photographed stage. After the base coat is ruled flat, a serrated scratcher is worked across the full surface — those marks you can see are the result. Polymer-modified base coats trap air during mixing and application; if left, those pockets blow as the coat cures, creating blisters and voids in the surface. The serrated edge breaks the skin and releases every trapped air bubble before they become a problem. Once the air is out, the surface is spat flat — closed back up with a float to give a tight, dense, bubble-free base. Skip this stage and you risk a surface that blisters and fails. It's not glamorous, but it's the kind of detail that separates a system that lasts 25 years from one that doesn't make 5.

Stage 02 / 22 Ruling Up
Stage 02i — Elevation Complete

Side Elevation — Ruled Up Flat

The full side elevation of the property after ruling up — flat, consistent, and uniform from top to bottom. The windows are still masked and protected. You can see how the base coat has transformed the raw substrate into a perfectly prepared surface ready for primer and topcoat. At this point the hard preparatory work is done. The standard of finish you see here at base coat stage is a direct reflection of the standard of the finished silicone coat — there are no shortcuts to a flat, clean result.

Stage 03 / 22 Primer
Stage 03 — Primer Application

Front Elevation Primed

The primer is on. Ecorend primer is applied by brush or roller across the full prepared base coat — this shot shows the front elevation now uniformly white. The primer does two things: it optimises adhesion between the base coat and the silicone topcoat, and it ensures consistent, even colour absorption across the full surface so the Marble White topcoat goes on true and uniform. You can see how the property is already being transformed — the white primer gives a clear preview of how the finished Ecorend Marble White coat will sit on the building. Windows still masked, scaffold still working.

Stage 03 / 22 Primer
Stage 03b — Primer Cured

Ready for the Topcoat

The primer fully cured in good Sheffield sunshine — the best possible conditions. This is the last stage before the Ecorend Marble White goes on. The surface is flat, white, and consistent from corner to corner across every elevation. The front of the property is almost unrecognisable from where it started — and the topcoat hasn't even gone on yet. The primer must be fully dry before the silicone is applied; applying topcoat over damp primer is one of the most common causes of adhesion failure. The patience at this stage is what gives you the 25-year result.

Stage 04 / 22 Topcoat
Stage 04 — Ecorend Marble White Application

Topcoat Going On — Front Elevation

This is the moment the job starts to look like the finished product. The Ecorend Marble White silicone topcoat is being applied across the front elevation — scaffold still standing, conditions good. The brilliant white going on over the primed base coat already shows the transformation underway. Ecorend Marble White is a 1.5mm scraped silicone render — pre-pigmented through-colour, self-cleaning, UV-stable, and hydrophobic. It goes on wet and is worked and scratched to produce the final texture finish. The colour you see going on here is the colour it stays — no paint, no coating on top, just the silicone itself.

Stage 04 / 22 Topcoat
Stage 04b — Texture Detail

1.5mm Scraped Texture — Up Close

The finest shot in the whole case study. A black wall light fitting sits flush against the Ecorend Marble White surface and you can read every detail of the 1.5mm scraped texture. This is what separates a premium silicone render from anything cheaper — the consistency of that texture across every square metre of the building. The 1.5mm aggregate creates a tight, even surface profile that catches light uniformly and looks as good in 20 years as it does today. The detailing around the light fitting — the tight junction, the clean edge — is the kind of finishing work that never shows up in a marketing brochure but is what you're actually paying for.

03Beading
Why is render beading so important?

Render beads are the framework the whole system hangs off. Stop beads control the render thickness and create clean edges at every window, door, corner, and base. Movement beads allow the system to flex with building movement. Without correct beading, the render will look uneven, the edges will crack, and the system will fail prematurely at its terminations. It's one of the most detail-intensive stages — and one of the first places a cheap contractor cuts corners.

06Product
What is the difference between silicone render and monocouche render?

Silicone render is a two-stage system: a polymer base coat with mesh reinforcement, followed by a thin silicone topcoat. It's flexible, self-cleaning, and highly hydrophobic — ideal for properties where longevity and low maintenance are the priority. Monocouche is a single-coat through-coloured render that goes straight onto the substrate — faster to apply but less flexible and without the same self-cleaning performance. For premium properties like this Sandygate job, silicone is the better long-term investment.

+
07Durability
Will silicone render crack?

A correctly specified and applied silicone render system is highly crack-resistant. The alkali-resistant fibreglass mesh embedded in the base coat provides structural reinforcement that absorbs building movement and thermal expansion. The silicone topcoat itself is inherently flexible. Cracking in render systems almost always comes down to poor preparation, missing movement beads, or skipping the mesh stage — none of which happen on a P&R Solutions job.

+
08Maintenance
Does silicone render need repainting?

No. Silicone render is through-coloured — the pigment runs through the full depth of the product, not just on the surface. There is no paint layer to fade, peel, or chalk. The Ecorend Marble White on this Sandygate property will look the same in 15 years as it does today, without any maintenance painting. That's the core advantage over painted or thin-coat systems.
